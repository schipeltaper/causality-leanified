Prerequisites

Before the course, the student should be able to:

• work with (elementary) probability theory, write down the main definitions and compute probabilities and expectation values;
• translate measure theoretic notations, like measure integrals or expectation values, into familiar ones in the discrete and absolute continuous cases;
• understand, memorize and explain the ’core’ definitions and constructions in Appendix A;
• work with the d-dimensional Euclidean space R as ’the’ standard measurable space;
• translate measure integrals to the two basic cases of either 1. discrete spaces/random variables/distributions (with probability mass functions) or 2. absolute continuous random variables (with probability densities w.r.t. Lebesgue measure) and compute those integrals (with the usual rules of anti-derivatives, etc.);
• understand and apply the transformation rules for discrete and absolute continuous variables to compute mass functions/densities of push-forward measures (in the absolute continuous case only for differentiable bijections);


Intended learning outcomes

After the course, the student should be able to:

Chapter 1:
• explain why correlation does not imply causation;
• explain 6 correlation inducing scenarios (e.g. cause-effect, effect-cause, feedback, confounding, selection bias, deterministic constraints, etc.);
• write down and explain the definition of causal effect (in real world terms);
• explain how one can detect causal relationships experimentally;
• explain randomized controlled trials and argue about advantages and disadvantages;
• explain the difference between (conditional) observational distributions and interventional distributions, when they are different, when they are  the same;
• analyse real world studies and claims according to the concepts introduced above.

Chapter 2:
• explain what a Markov kernel is and how to represent them in the discrete and absolute continuous case;
• give examples of Markov kernels;
• properly work with discrete Markov kernels and linear Gaussians ones;
• explain how Markov kernels relate to deterministic maps and probability distributions;
• explain transition probability spaces and null-sets;
• explain and work with the 5 constructions of Markov kernels from others (composition, product, marginal, conditional, push-forward) and how to work with them in the discrete and absolute continuous case;
• Write down the definition of conditional independence for conditional random variables and work with them in the discrete and continuous cases;
• Write down the separoid axioms for conditional independence of conditional random variables;
• prove separoid axioms for random variables in the discrete and continuous case (in RD);
• explain how one can get a given Markov kernel by a deterministic mapping and a probability distribution.

Chapter 3
• write down the definition of CDMGs and explain the meaning;
• explain all the different concepts of walks (directed, bi-directed, collider, fork, path, etc.);
• explain all the different node/edge relationships (parents, children, etc.);
• explain the notion of acyclicity;
• write down the definition of topological order and how it relates to acyclicity;
• explain the different operations on graphs (hard/soft interventions, marginalization);
• write down the definition of d-separation and check if sets are d-separated in given graphs;
• write down the most important separoid axioms for d-separation, reason with them and prove them;
• know and understand the definition of σ-separation;
• check if sets are σ-separated in given graphs.

Chapter 4
• write down the definition of causal Bayesian network with input and latent variables, motivate the meaning and reason about them;
• work with the operations on causal Bayesian networks, like hard interventions, soft interventions, marginalization;
• precisely state the global Markov property, write it down, reason about it and apply it to different causal Bayesian networks where the graph is given;
• know the main ingredients and steps for the proof of the global Markov property (not necessarily every single step);
• write down what interventional equivalence means and reason about it;
• write down how a standard forms (SCM form, canonical form) of causal Bayesian network looks like and reason about it.

Chapter 5
• write down the 3 do-calculus rules and reason with them;
• prove the 3 rules of do-calculus using the global Markov property;
• apply the 3 rules of do-calculus in an iterative manner on (small, not too complicated) graphs for the identification of causal effects (i.e. reducing the number do-terms);
• state and apply the back-door covariate adjustment criterion and formula;
• explain the general idea of the ID-algorithm.

Chapter 6
• know and understand the definition of structural causal model (SCM);
• know the differences between endogenous variables, exogenous input variables and exogenous random variables;
• understand the implicit assumptions made when using a SCM;
• know and understand the definitions of (potential) outcomes, solutions, their Markov kernels, and solution functions of SCMs;
• know how to sample from a SCM;
• know and understand the definitions of the different variants of hard interventions on SCMs;
• understand how soft interventions can be modeled in SCMs;
• understand how intervention variables can be used to model different interventions in the same causal model;
• understand how to compose and decompose SCMs;
• propose an appropriate SCM for a given real-world system, and to appropriately model interventions on the system using the model;
• know and understand the definition and consequences of unique solvability for SCMs;
• know and understand the definition and consequences of an SCM being simple;
• be able to check whether a given SCM is uniquely solvable/simple;
• construct observable and intervened Markov kernels for simple SCMs;
• understand the different equivalence relations for SCMs (see also Chapter 8);
• know and understand the definition of marginalization of SCMs;
• construct a marginalized SCM;
• know and apply the basic commutation and compatibility properties of basic operations on SCMs (intervening, marginalization, preservation of unique solvability / simplicity / equivalence);
• know and understand the definition of the graph of an SCM;
• be able to draw the graph of a given SCM, and to construct an SCM with a given graph;
• know and apply the basic compatibility relations between the operation that maps an SCM to a graph and other basic SCM operations;
• give a causal interpretation of the graph of an SCM (see Chapter 9);
• know the definition of acyclic SCM;
• understand and appreciate the differences between different causal model classes.

Chapter 7
• know and understand the definitions of acyclification of a graph and of a simple SCM, and their relation;
• know the relation between σ-separation and d-separation on an acyclification;
• know and understand the global Markov property for simple SCMs;
• apply the global Markov property for simple SCMs;
• know and understand the 3 do-calculus rules for simple SCMs;
• apply the 3 do-calculus rules for simple SCMs;
• state and apply the back-door covariate adjustment criterion and formula for simple SCMs.

Chapter 8
• construct a twin SCM;
• construct a twin graph;
• know and understand the basic commutation and compatibility properties of twinning with other basic operations on and properties of SCMs;
• be able to reason about counterfactuals by using the twin SCM;
• be aware of the empirical limitations of working with counterfactuals.

Chapter 9
• know and understand the definitions of indirect causal relations, direct causal relations, and common causes according to simple SCMs;
• understand the relation between having a common cause and being confounded;
• express the notion of “no confounding” in terms of potential outcomes;
• know how to detect the presence of such relations from Markov kernels induced by simple SCMs;
• deal formally with a situation in which only part of the variables in an SCM are observed, whereas the others are latent;
• formally model a randomized controlled trial with SCMs in various ways, and prove in the SCM formalism the main properties of RCTs;
• know and understand the definition of faithfulness;
• understand different ways of how faithfulness violations can occur;
• understand the LCD algorithm;
• prove the correctness of the LCD algorithm;
• understand the Y-structures approach to causal discovery;
• prove the correctness of the Y-structures approach to causal discovery;
• know how minimal separating/connecting sets relate to ancestral relations.

Chapter 10
• understand how to use the G test for conditional independence;
• understand under which conditions the G test is valid (at level α);
• understand under which conditions the G test is asymptotically consistent;
• implement the G test in a computer program;

Chapter 11
• know and understand the definition of σ-inducing path;
• know and understand the definition of a σ-PAG;
• know which CDMGs a σ-PAG represents;
• construct a σ-PAG representing a given CDMG.
