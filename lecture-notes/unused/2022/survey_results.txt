-----

A. I wanted to learn more about causal models and the (mathematical) formalization of causality as a phenomenon. I also thought this to be a well taught course (given the lecturer's background).

B. It does teach a lot (both in depth and breadth) about causality, which is fortunate. The lectures are very well-structured. I am generally happy about the course.

C. I am however surprised by the level of measure theoretical involvement in the subject. I suspected a more state-space model/graph theory/Markov chains kind of course. 

I also think that the amount of homework assignments is high for a 6pnt (2 period) course, especially since it is optional for many students (except SFM/Math MSc students). Likewise, I think a maximum of 2 or 3 assignments to be more suitable for a weekly hand-in than the current work-load.

Some additional practical examples more aligned with the assignments could help, since there are quite a few challenging theoretical concepts throughout the course. It is helpful if homework/exam-problems are to some extent 1 to 1 with relatively easy examples you have seen that had been solved in the lectures. Many courses instead are poorly helpful in instructing how to apply taught theory to problems (not per se this course).

D. I will continue to participate, but the amount of work for other courses (mandatory courses) ensures that I don't have time to deliver the homework assignments in period 4. I will likely catch up in period 5 and miss points for the assignment in period 4.

E. As an econometrics MSc student, I really like this course. I hope you can keep it as an optional course for non-mathematics majors. But do warn them about the heavy reliance on measure theory and the need for mathematical maturity. 

Perhaps splitting the course into two 3 point courses could be considered. Then it is possible to focus one period - say period 4 - on topics in causality that are more suitable for AI/CS/Econometrics/Computational science majors. Then a second follow-up course (period 5) could be more explicitly mathematical and measure theoretical, which would be interesting to people familiar with/interested in that level of mathematical depth.
