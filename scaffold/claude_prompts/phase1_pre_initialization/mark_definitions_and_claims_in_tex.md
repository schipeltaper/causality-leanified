Go through the current chapter, and I want you to identify all definitions and claims.

When you find a definition you can write \begin{defmark} at the start of the definition
and \end{defmark} at the end within the Tex files. And the same for any claim: \begin{claimmark} and \end{claimmark}.

An example for a definition would be:
\begin{defmark}
\begin{Def}[Standard measurable space, see \cite{Fremlin} 424A-G]
 A measurable space $(\Wcal,\Bcal_\Wcal)$ is called \emph{standard measurable space} (aka standard Borel space) if it is measurably isomorphic to either:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item a finite measurable space $\{1,\dots,M\}$ for some $M \in \N$ endowed with the power set $\sigma$-algebra $2^{\{1,\dots,M\}}$, or:
        \item the countably infinite space $\N$ endowed with the power set $\sigma$-algebra $2^\N$, or:
        \item the unit interval $[0,1]$ endowed with its Borel $\sigma$-algebra\footnote{See Definition \ref{def-sigma-algebra-gen} for the $\sigma$-algebra generated by a set of subsets.}:
           \[\Bcal_{[0,1]} =\sigma\lp\lC [a,b] \st a, b \in [0,1] \cap \Q, a \le b \rC \rp.\]
    \end{enumerate}
    ``Measurably isomorphic'' means that there is a measurable map from one space to the other that has a measurable inverse.
\end{Def}
\end{defmark}

And an example for a claim would be
\begin{claimmark}
\begin{Thm}[Kuratowski et al., see \cite{Fremlin} 424A-G]
        Every Borel subset of any complete metric space that has a countable dense subset is a standard measurable space in its Borel $\sigma$-algebra.
\end{Thm}
\end{claimmark}

## Purpose
We want to Leanify this chapter. And we want to stay very close to the lecture notes. The plan will be first spot every definition and claim, and then leanify them all. For your purposes just try to be VERY precise. Spot them all!

## What counts as a definition?
Anything that is noted as \begin{Def} ... \end{Def} in the tex files. But also for notation it is nice to create something in Lean. We want to stay close to the Lean files, so anything that would require a definition or structure in Lean suffices

## What counts as a claim?
Every theorem, remark, lemma, corollary, etc. But it could also be a small note in a paragraph. Or a small note within a definition. Basically, EVERY claim in the broadest sense of the word. Spot them ALL.

## Extra notes
These marks that you add can be in the middle of a paragraph too. Sometimes a claim is hidden in a paragraph. Even, sometimes a claim is within a definition. So they can also be inside each other.
